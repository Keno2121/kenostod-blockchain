PART 1: Flash Arbitrage Loans (FAL)
Active trading — spot price discrepancies across exchanges and capture profits in single atomic transactions

📈Arbitrage Fundamentals

Arbitrage is the practice of simultaneously buying and selling the same asset in different markets to profit from price differences. In traditional finance, arbitrage opportunities close within milliseconds as traders exploit them. In crypto markets, inefficiencies last longer due to fragmented liquidity across dozens of exchanges.

Why Price Differences Exist

Every exchange operates as its own independent marketplace. Prices differ because of:

  • Liquidity differences — high-volume exchanges have tighter spreads
  • Order book depth — large trades move prices differently on thin books
  • Price update latency — slower exchanges lag behind market-moving news
  • Geographic trading patterns — regional demand affects local exchange prices

📈 Real Example: Binance vs. Coinbase

KENO trades at 1.050 on Binance and 1.071 on Coinbase — a 2% spread. A trader borrows 1,000 KENO via FAL, buys on Binance for 1,050, sells on Coinbase for 1,071, repays the loan, and keeps the $21 profit minus platform fees. All in one atomic transaction.

💡Flash Loans Explained

A flash loan is a loan where you borrow, use, and repay funds all within a single atomic transaction. If repayment fails, the entire transaction reverts — meaning the lender never actually loses the principal. This makes zero-collateral lending possible.

How Atomic Transactions Enable Flash Loans

Blockchain transactions are either fully completed or fully reverted. A flash loan wraps the entire sequence — borrow → trade → repay — into one atomic block. If step 3 (repayment) fails, steps 1 and 2 also reverse automatically. The lender's capital is never at risk.

⚙FAL Mechanics & Execution

Executing a Flash Arbitrage Loan on Kenostod follows a structured process. Understanding each step prevents costly mistakes.

The FAL Execution Flow

  • Identify Opportunity
    Monitor price feeds across Binance, Coinbase, Kraken, and KuCoin. Look for spreads >1.5% to clear fees.
  • Request FAL
    Submit a loan request for the KENO amount within your tier limit. No collateral needed — your reputation is the collateral.
  • Execute Trade
    Buy on the low-price exchange and sell on the high-price exchange within the execution window.
  • Repay + Fees
    Repay principal plus platform fee (typically 0.1–0.3%). Transaction reverts automatically if repayment fails.
  • Keep Profit
    Net profit = price spread − platform fee − gas cost. Successful trades improve your reputation tier.

💰Profit Calculation & Break-Even

The Formula

Net Profit = (Sell Price − Buy Price) × Quantity − (Platform Fee + Gas Cost)
Break-even spread % = (Platform Fee + Gas Cost) ÷ Loan Amount × 100

Worked Example

📈 2% Spread on 1,000 KENO Trade

  • Buy on Exchange A: 1,000 KENO × $1.00 = $1,000
  • Sell on Exchange B: 1,000 KENO × $1.02 = $1,020
  • Gross profit: $20
  • Platform fee (0.1%): $1.00
  • Gas cost: ~$0.50
  • Net profit: $18.50 per trade

At Diamond tier with 10,000 KENO: the same 2% spread yields $185+ per transaction.

⚠FAL Risk Management

Key Risks Every FAL Trader Faces

Slippage
When prices move during your execution window. A 2% spread can disappear if Exchange B's price drops 2% before your sell order fills. Mitigate by setting max acceptable slippage parameters.
Liquidity Risk
Thin order books on smaller exchanges may not absorb your full trade at the expected price. Always check order book depth before committing to a large loan.
Transaction Failure
If repayment fails, the entire transaction reverts. No loss to the trader — but you waste gas fees. Failed trades do not count toward reputation progress.
Timing Decay
Arbitrage opportunities close fast. During high-volatility periods, spreads can disappear in seconds. Speed of execution is critical.
⚠ Never Over-Extend

Only take loans sized for opportunities you've already verified. Chasing phantom spreads by taking maximum-limit loans is the most common beginner mistake.

⚙FALP Pool Mechanics

Contributing to a FALP pool is straightforward. You deposit KENO, choose your lock period, and start earning a share of arbitrage profits proportional to your contribution.

What Happens to Your Deposited KENO

  • Your KENO joins the pool
    Your deposit increases the pool's Total Value Locked (TVL), making larger FAL loans available.
  • FAL traders borrow from the pool
    Each loan pays a platform fee (0.1–0.3%) upon repayment. These fees accumulate in the pool.
  • Profits distributed to contributors
    Distributions occur daily or weekly. Your share is proportional to your contribution and lock period bonus.
  • Compound by reinvesting
    Reinvest your distributions back into the pool to accelerate growth through compounding.

🔨Profit Distribution Formula

The Core Formula

Your Share = (Your Contribution ÷ Total Pool) × Total Pool Profits × Lock Bonus

Worked Example

🌊 Pool Calculation

  • You deposit: 5,000 KENO
  • Total pool size: 100,000 KENO
  • Your share: 5,000 ÷ 100,000 = 5%
  • Pool generates 2,000 KENO in arbitrage fees this week
  • Your base share: 5% × 2,000 = 100 KENO
  • 90-day lock bonus: 100 × 1.35 = 135 KENO this week
  • Annualized: 135 × 52 weeks = ~7,020 KENO/year on 5,000 staked (140% APY)

🔍Pool Evaluation & Selection

What to Look at Before Committing KENO

Historical APY
Check the pool's 30, 60, and 90-day average APY. Consistent performance is better than one spike followed by drops.
Total Value Locked (TVL)
Larger TVL means more capital to fund bigger trades. Too small = limited opportunities. Too large = diluted returns. Sweet spot is mid-sized, growing pools.
Pool Manager Reputation
Experienced managers have high FAL trade success rates. Look for pools managed by Diamond-tier traders.
Utilization Rate
What percentage of pool capital is actively deployed in loans? 60–80% is healthy. <30% means underperforming capital. >95% means insufficient liquidity buffer.

Diversifying Across Pools

Don't concentrate all KENO in one pool. A common strategy: 50% in one large Conservative pool, 30% in a Balanced pool, 20% in a smaller Aggressive pool for upside exposure.
